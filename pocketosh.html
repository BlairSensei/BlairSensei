pocketOSH

iOS Application | student project


Client: Fellows Robots

Duration: Two Week Sprint

Team: Catherine Harrell and Jon Infranco

My Role: screener and interview script writing, competitive and comparitive analysis, interviews, affinity diagramming, persona creation, sketching, storyboarding, wireframes, user interface design, rapid prototyping, user testing

Tools: Axure, Sketch, Omnigraffle, Illustrator


Prototype

The Challenge

Fellows Robots, a robotics company at the forefront of incorporating robotic assistants into the retail space, has developed a fully autonomous robot called NAVii. These robots give customers information about products, tell you where they are located in stores, and lead you directly to the product. Unfortunately, Fellow Robots currently has no mobile presence and would like a way for customers to learn how to use NAVii in a retail environment, in particular OSHbot. OSHbots are the robots working in Orchard Supply Hardware home improvement and gardening stores.


While the OSHbot robot is quite simple to use, many people do not have much experience interacting with robots. A number of people that were interviewed had reservations using such a robot. Because this is a quite new experience for many people, having a simple way to educate people about OSHbots could increase the number of people interacting with them.

The Approach

First my team and I did organizational and domain research. While we didn't have a chance to interact with an OSHbot, we did call a store that has one and got some very good information. Interview screeners were then sent out to find the people we were looking to interview. My colleagues and I compiled the data from the interviews we conducted and began making artifacts to make sense of all the information we gathered.


Persona

From this data we were made two personas, the primary persona being Olivia.


Scenario

My team and I created a scenario for Olivia which I later illustrated.

Olivia gets home after a long day at the office. She had to deal with a lot more clients than normal today and is really looking forward to relaxing in front of the TV this evening.


When she gets home and unlocks the door she remembers she was supposed to go to Orchard Supply Hardware to replace her husband's lost key. As she is leaving she notices a couple lights out and adds them to the shopping list.


As she drives to the store, she dreads the thought of more human interaction today.

When she enters the store, she realized she has no clue where to go and that this trip to the store will take a lot longer than she hoped.


The Solution

We then held a charrette (or design studio) to help us focus our efforts on the structure and functionality of the app. It was during this design studio that we had one of our big breakthroughs with the app. Many people were weary of interacting with a customer service robot, so we knew we had to use the pocketOSH app as a means for the customers to “dip their toes in the water”. My idea was to have the OSHbot robot and pocketOSH app “talk” to each other through the use of speech bubbles. We hoped that by giving the app and robot personality, more people would be open to using the OSHbot robot in the store.

Prototype

Wireframes and our first iteration of the prototype were made in Axure. I made most of the navigation and icons for the pages and was in charge of illustrating and setting up the "About OSHbot" page on the app. User testing of the prototype proved to be very useful. One big issue we had was people had trouble understanding that the pocketOSH app and OSHbot robot were two different entities in the "About OSHbot" section of the app. The dialog was simplified to make it more clear, and I changed the speech bubbles to display in a way that made it seem more like a back-and-forth conversation.


Testing

Most people were able to learn about the features of OSHbot by using the pocketOSH application. There was still a bit of confusion about the navigation bar and some of the other navigation within the products and services pages. More card sorting, site map revisions and UI changes may be needed to improve the user flow through the app.


Olivia's Solution

Thanks to pocketOSH, Olivia has now found that shopping may not be as terrible as she once believed. Perhaps she would even consider it enjoyable.

Next Steps

Further interviews and user testing will be needed to see if a wider range of people are being helped by the app and learning how to interact with OSHbot from it.


The number of searches being conducted on the app, rate of people using the OSHbot robot, as well as other success metrics must be taken into account.

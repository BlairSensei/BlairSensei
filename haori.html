The Project


The global pandemic. Most of us were stuck indoors for months with not much to do. In my boredom, I started getting the itch to start sewing again. I had started getting into wearing kimono, so I decided to make a jacket worn over a kimono called a haori.

Here we go again...

A number of years ago, I made a quilt. I was an absolute nightmare. It took forever to make the design, create the templates, measure material, cut out the material, and sew it all together. The material was also expensive. So of course I had a brilliant idea: What if I turned making a haori (a fairly simple piece of clothing) into another hellish sewing project? How about instead of sewing six rectangles of material together to complete it, I design a complicated, patchwork pattern made of hundreds of pieces, then sewed it all together? So that is what I did. I basically ended up deciding to make a wearable quilt. To make it even harder for myself, I choose to make it entirely out of denim, which is not light or easy to sew.

I began by sketching out design ideas for the patchwork pattern of the haori using half square triangles (a quilting term). I measured a haori I have to get an idea of the measurements of the fabric panels. Once I had a few pattern ideas, I moved over to the computer to finalize the pattern. This was a very important step, as it gave me an idea of how many different materials I was going to use, and how much material I would need to purchase. I ultimately decided on four different denim materials.

Prep Work

I bought my material and then began to measure out and draw the chalk lines. As I needed 360 triangles to make my design, this took a while. About five and a half hours. After that was done, I cut them out with sheers. That took about another five hours.

To The Machine

Then it was finally time to jump on the sewing machine. Following my design closely, I started by making sets of six different material combinations of triangles to make my squares. I then started sewing the squares together by panels, constantly checking my design (making mistakes and having to rip out threads is not fun). I completed the main panels and realized I really had not thought much about how to put the panels together. As I am a self taught sewer, I just winged it and got it sewn together somehow.


Since the inside face of the patchwork is not that nice to look at, I made an inner liner for the haori. This was basically a second haori that I stuck on the inside of the first one. I made this of black denim, since the indigo denim I buy will turn any material it rubs against blue. The black denim will not transfer dye, and will protect whatever I wear under it. After that I attached the collar, got rid of any exposed threads, and tried to fix/hide any mistakes I found.

Results

All in all, it took around forty to fifty hours to complete the haori. It weighs around 6.6 pounds (3 kilograms), and is too hot to wear in any season other than winter. If you rub your hands on the side when you walk around, your hands will turn blue. I am pretty happy with how it turned out.
